Claude Audit: Validation of Shortage Index

Classification of 872 News Article Abstracts from Historical Newspapers (1900-2024)

Overview

This report documents an audit performed using Claude AI (Claude 3.7 Sonnet reasoning model) to validate the accuracy of our shortage index. We sampled 872 article abstracts from the shortage set and classified whether each abstract mentions actual, economically relevant shortages.

Purpose: Verify that our index accurately measures shortages, minimizing both Type-I and Type-II errors.

Sample: 872 abstracts from articles in the shortage set (𝒮), drawn from six major U.S. newspapers until 2014, and expanded to include British, Canadian, and Australian newspapers for 2015-2024.

Note: Abstracts in the database are typically short portions of text, often containing the opening sentences or the initial paragraphs of the article. A list of the 872 abstracts is available upon request.

Methodology

Snippet Extraction

For each article, we extracted the first snippet of text that references shortages. These snippets are centered on the shortage word and limited to 110 characters—a length chosen to balance brevity with providing sufficient context.

Example Snippet:

"...Ford motor company said today that still lingering steel shortages would force a closing next week of eighteen..."
(New York Times, 1952)

Classification Scheme

Claude was instructed to classify each snippet using a three-level system:

  • 1 (Yes): Mentions current or prospective shortages, rationing, scarcity, or bottlenecks related to industry, labor, materials, food, or water
  • 0 (No): Does not mention relevant economic shortages (e.g., mentions end of shortages, or non-economic uses like "shortage of sunshine")
  • 99 (Unsure): Ambiguous or unclear classification

Training Examples Provided to Claude

Before classification, we provided Claude with examples to ensure proper understanding, including false positives:

  • "Steel shortages will prevail in the near future" → 1 (mentions prospective shortages)
  • "Steel shortages caused a plant closure" → 1 (mentions current shortage impacts)
  • "No shortage of cars has been experienced" → 0 (mentions absence of shortage)
  • "Shortage of workspace" (not work shortage) → 0 (not economically relevant)
  • "Shortage of frigid weather" → 0 (figurative, not economic)
  • "Shortage of funds" from personal account → 0 (not goods/services shortage)

Claude's Performance

Although AI-based validation is not foolproof, Claude performed comparably to a human evaluator, even extrapolating contextual cues. For example, when processing the snippet "Economy may be slowing but Lowe is banking on labour shortages gradually leading to an increase...", Claude classified it as 1 and noted that Philip Lowe (Governor of the Reserve Bank of Australia) was expecting labor shortages to lead to wage growth.

Implications for Index Validity

The audit results validate that our shortage index accurately captures articles mentioning economically relevant shortages. The low false positive rate (6.30%) demonstrates that our search methodology—requiring shortage words to appear near topic words (energy, food, industry, labor)—effectively filters out irrelevant uses of shortage terminology.

What the False Positives Reveal

The 55 articles classified as false positives (6.30%) primarily fall into three categories:

  • Negations: Articles explicitly stating there is "no shortage" or shortages have ended
  • Non-economic shortages: Figurative uses (e.g., "shortage of sunshine") or non-goods/services contexts (e.g., "shortage of political campaign funds")
  • Context-dependent edge cases: Mentions of shortages that, upon closer reading, refer to hypothetical or counterfactual scenarios rather than actual conditions

These false positives represent measurement noise that is both minimal and random, unlikely to introduce systematic bias into our index.
